3
Limits
Derived in Empathetic Relationship

Society can only offer agency predictably if not subjected to the kinds of existential risks of catastrophe introduced by management by distant economies and institutions. Affectionate and empathetic relationship with the people and places around us can help more naturally diffuse these risks.

Imperceptive institutional management doesn't just produce disengaged people in the present, but compounding externalities and broader existential risks over longer time horizons. In contrast, relationally grounded economies and their organizational systems help produce locally appropriate limits, derived in affection, that make places around us more resilient and stable over time.

3.1 Unknown Dependencies, Unknown Limits
▼

The more economic systems decouple from locally anchored relationship, the more likely we are to depend on resources that come from outside our communities. If some communities are consuming beyond their local means, it is increasingly likely that others will be producing beyond their local means. This builds pathways for exploitation into our economic system, and our ‘best’ places become the most dependent on this exploitation.

Impersonal economies have strong incentives against  managing their risk. Altering a supply chain is inconvenient and expensive, while internalizing tragedy  that occurs outside your community is difficult . This inability to metabolize sorrow at a distance is known as psychic numbing .

“In today’s networked world, our economies falsely treat immaterial resources (knowledge / designs) as if they were scarce through restrictive global intellectual property regimes, and treat material resources (minerals, soils, water) as if they were abundant.”

- José Ramos, Action Foresight

Many of the risks of long, just-in-time supply chains have been made more plain  over the last several years. The average globally-minded manufacturer exists in a specific physical space so long as costs make sense, but will move on to greener pastures if financially prudent, even if they are themselves responsible for local pastures becoming less green .

On the other side of the transaction, consumers increasingly live nowhere near the place their food (or product) comes from, and thus are not faced with the implications of its creation. Sadly, in the places producing beyond their means, there are fewer and fewer  loving stewards and more only-economically-present landholders .

Scaled Institutions Reinforce Scaled Economies

In acknowledgement of a need to account for accumulating externalities and existential risks, institutions step in to enforce limits, often with plenty of room for regulatory capture  and arbitrage. To produce defensible limits, regulators employ tools of statistical analysis  that perpetuate patterns of exploitation and scale through their preference for legibility. In other words, institutional intervention as we know it today, without greater extension of trust to local communities, only serves to reinforce the trajectory of compounding externalities.

Bureaucratic regulation itself generates further complexity and costs. As regulations are issued and taxes established, those who are regulated or taxed seek loopholes and lawmakers strive to close these. A competitive spiral of loophole discovery and closure unfolds, with complexity continuously increasing.

- Mancur Olson , The Rise and Decline of Nations

3.2 Real Consequences
▼

Over time, governance disconnected from local places tends towards an insidious burden of deferred maintenance  on communities who were entirely uninvolved in the decisions that got them there. This is what happens when the health of places is predicated on growth, and when new large projects that look good on paper are prioritized over iterative improvement and maintenance.

Our country’s transportation maintenance burden , for example, is insurmountable precisely because no one will take responsibility for it . It keeps expanding, however, because tax dollars from new construction, subsidized by state and federal transportation authorities, are needed to pay for less-flashy maintenance that doesn’t win federal funding.

Local people won’t know how to get out of these situations if they don’t know how they got there , but they’ll still be on the hook , especially in the case of effects on the land  and people’s health  that outlast the companies that create them. Lost lives and abandoned communities can’t be remedied by more federal dollars .

When most municipal projects are funded by debt financed on the assumption of growth, the result is unfortunate but inevitable. Predicated on growth, our organizational structures make us indebted to the future, and sometimes the future can't pay off the debt.

There are also risks of certain outcomes in economies of scale that are less predictable but more catastrophic. These more violent collapse  or unwinding events are called ‘black swans’. Nassim Taleb  has been going out of his way to warn of these risks  for decades. Existential risks of an enthusiastic embrace of Artificial Intelligence  are top of the list these days. Not surprisingly AI is a perfect kind of agency-revoking machine.

"Once a complex society develops the vulnerabilities of declining marginal returns, collapse may merely require sufficient passage of time to render probable the occurrence of an insurmountable calamity."

- Joseph Tainter , The Collapse of Complex Societies

Large scale systems that rest on relatively few fulcrums mean bigger failures, and less mechanisms for awareness and incremental correction. Unaccounted-for-externalities compound into very real , but unpredictable consequences. This phenomenon is not a new one, Ibn Khaldun described  non-agentic dependence on outside resources as a precusor to societal collapse as early as the 1300s in one the very first  sociological texts.

“In the 7th century AD, it looked like the Byzantine Empire was going to fail… the solution that they adopted was that they gave up their professional army, which was very expensive, and they settled soldiers on plots of land across the empire where they became a peasant militia providing for their own sustenance rather than having to be paid by the government.

The peasant soldiers suddenly began fighting better because they were fighting for their own land and they were fighting for their own families, so they had more incentive to fight… Within 200 years they had reconquered much of the land they had lost, and they were now the premier power of Europe and the Near East. This was an accomplishment that they won by simplifying, by reducing the complexity and the costliness of their problem solving institutions.

In energetic terms, they simplified their energy flows. One can think of this in energy terms, at each intermediate step [of complex institutions], there is some loss. There is a cost to the transaction.

- Joseph Tainter

3.3 Limits Derived in Empathetic Relationship
▼

As we’ve discussed, the tendency of industrial economies to produce existential risk can be remedied by local, more sensitive structures of organizing. These structures don’t accumulate externalities, they match inputs to outputs in cyclic  patterns that conserve energy and reduce external impact. Natural systems build health, not in their accumulation of resources, but in the quality of active relationships.

“A sustainable system, maximizes cyclic flows. Activities yielding energy are directly coupled to those requiring energy...This dynamic is based on symbiosis, cooperation and reciprocity.  It is fair and just reciprocal exchange of materials and energy that maintains and ensures the survival of the whole.” - Mae-Wan Ho,

- Circular Thermodynamics of Organisms and Sustainable Systems

This concept of sustainable non-dissipative structures  that are sensitive to local ecological systems goes a long way in helping us understand why locally coherent systems, like a closed terrarium , are more resilient than growth-hungry patterns of consume-and-dispose energy use.

The structure of our community and economic organization, then, is more important than its scale. While our linear  economies hasten destruction as they scale, the energy-efficient reciprocal cycles of healthy forests improve with scale and complexity . Scale must include concentric endogenous systems or it is not efficient except in its velocity towards death.

Iteration is the Opposite of Catastrophe

For true architecture grows out of normal building activity, and this normal building activity is the organic development of a shell around a group of people. Such building growth is like the growth of a child and of man.

- Friedensreich Hundertwasser

Relational organizational structures, with high degrees of local sovereignty  and reciprocity  create iteratively adaptive, living places attuned to their local environment. This rate of change is more compatible with social and ecological abilities to digest change  than our current model  of build, rot, replace  communities and boom or bust  economies.

In comparing iterative organization, rich in nested cyclic patterns with master planned communities, all degrading at the same time, it is clear our relationship to time is an important consideration in healthy societies. Next, we’ll try to understand this relationship in chapter 2 before getting into specific technologies that may aid us in maintaining these healthy organizational structures in chapter 3.

Always in human history there have been costly or catastrophic sudden changes. But, with relentless fanfare, at the cost of ecological & social disorder, and to the enrichment and empowerment of corporations, industrialists have substituted what they fairly accurately call "revolution" for the slower, kinder processes of adaptation or evolution. We have had in only about two centuries a steady and ever-quickening sequence of industrial revolutions in manufacturing, transportation, war, agriculture, education, entertainment, homemaking and family life, health care, and so-called communities. All of these changes have depended upon industrial technologies, processes, & products, which have depended upon the fossil fuels.. and the cycle of obsolescence and innovation, goaded by crazes of fashion, has given the corporate economy a controlling share of everybody's income. But what technology can replace personal privacy or the coherence of a family or a community? What technology can undo the collateral damages of an inhuman rate of technological change?

- Wendell Berry, It All Turns on Affection
